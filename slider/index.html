Olympic Sports: Curling

    • Curling has been an Olympic sport since 1998 but the strategy of the game remains a mystery to many spectators.

    • The Basics of Curling

    Curling is a sport in which two teams of four players each slide 40-pound granite rocks, or "stones" down a sheet of ice toward a target at the other end. The objective is to get more stones closer to the center of the target than the other team.

    Each player on a team throws two stones each round, or "end", alternating throws with the opposing team. Depending on the rotation when thrown, the stone may curl, or bend, as it travels down the ice. Sweeping the ice with a broom makes the rock curl less and travel farther.

    It is a game of etiquette and strategy. A game is always preceded by a handshake and the expression, "Good curling," giving the sport the nickname of "Chess On Ice."

    LEAD

    The Lead is the first to throw the stone down the "sheet", or lane, of ice. This player must then sweep the ice for all other team members' shots.

    SECOND

    The Second, intuitively, throws second, and then sweeps all other team members' shots. These shots are more difficult because there are already stones in play, acting as obstacles.

    VICE

    The Vice throws third, sweeps the throws of the Lead and Second, and "holds brush", or directs the line of the Skip's throw from the opposite end of the sheet.

    SKIP

    The Skip is the team captain and throws last. This player holds brush for all other players, tells other players when to sweep, and observes the "curl" of the throws. The Skip has the most difficult shots because all other stones have been played.

    Curling stones are made from a unique type of granite that is mined exclusively in Scotland, where the sport originated. They weigh approximately 42 pounds and cost approximately $1000 when purchased new.

    Curling is played on level ice, but the ice has small "pebbles", or droplets of water, that create friction and cause the stone to curl.

    A stone is delivered from the hack and must be released before the stone reaches the closer hog line. A player can re-throw if the stone did not reach the closer tee line.

    DRAW

    A stone that lands in the house

    GUARD

    A stone that lands short of the house and either protects a stone in the house or is put up first and then curled around later

    TAKEOUT

    A stone with sufficient "weight" to go through the house

    • Sweeping + Scoring

    Sweeping can make a stone go about 16 feet further by making it curl less and run straighter by cleaning away any debris in front of the stone. Once a stone has been thrown, there is no way to slow it down or make it curl more. A player can sweep their teams's stone as soon as it is thrown until it stops.

    A stone must land in the house, which is a combination of circles with a total diameter of 12 feet, to count for points. A stone must cross the hog line but go no further than the back line to still be in play.

    The team with the most points after all 16 stones have been thrown (2 stones for 8 players) wins the "end", which is similar to an inning in baseball. Only one team can score in an end and there are a total of 10 ends played in the Olympics per match. The team with the most ends wins!
